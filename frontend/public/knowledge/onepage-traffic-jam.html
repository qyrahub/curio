Where is the traffic jam?

One page. One idea: a struggling child usually isn't failing at "thinking" — they're stuck at one specific stage of it. Find the stage, and you find the fix.

The three stages of thinking

What you're looking at

Reuven Feuerstein's insight was that intelligence isn't fixed — it's modifiable. And that when a child gets stuck, the stuckness has an address. Information flows through three stages, and a breakdown at any one of them produces the same disappointing mark on the page, for entirely different reasons.

Input — did it land?

Focus, systematic searching, precision, holding two things at once. A child who half-heard the instruction never had a chance at the rest.

Elaboration — did they work it?

Defining the problem, sifting relevant from irrelevant, comparing, working memory, planning, summing up. This is thinking proper.

Output — did it come out cleanly?

Precision, self-control, perseverance, copying accurately. A child can know the answer and still lose it on the way out.

The reframe

Don't ask “Why aren't you getting this?” Ask “Where is the traffic jam?”

The gold band underneath is the part that does the work: mediation. Not marking, not re-explaining louder — but stepping in beside the child and lending them your thinking until it becomes theirs. That's the difference between a corrected worksheet and a built capability.

Try it tonight
  • Check the input: “Let's read it together. What is this question actually asking?”
  • Guide the elaboration: “What's our plan? What can we safely ignore?”
  • Refine the output: “Read it back to me — does it answer the question?”
