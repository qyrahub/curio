Reading a worksheet the way Curio does

A marked worksheet is one of the richest signals you'll get about your child — if you know how to read it.

For ParentsDiscipline SchoolRead 5 min

Most of us glance at a marked worksheet, register the score, and move on. But the score is the least interesting thing on the page. What the work actually shows — how a child approached the task, where they went off course, what they clearly grasped — is a far better guide to what to do next. Here is how to read it deliberately.

Separate the two voices

On most schoolwork there are two voices: the printed text, which is the teacher's instruction or the task, and the handwriting, which is the child's own answer. Read them as a pair. The real question is not "did they get it right?" but "did they do what was actually asked?"

If the instruction says "write down the six differences" and the child wrote four, that is not a comprehension failure — it is a following-instructions signal. If it asks for full sentences and the child gave fragments, the gap is about form, not knowledge. Naming which kind of gap it is changes what you do about it.

Look at how they went wrong

Two children can get the same answer wrong for completely different reasons. One misread the passage; one understood it perfectly but spells phonetically; one knew the answer but ran out of patience and rushed the last three. The mark is identical; the fix is not.

The mistake is the message. Read it for its shape, not just its size.

Cite specifics as you go — "she answered the first four in full sentences and then switched to single words" tells you exactly where stamina ran out. Vague impressions ("needs to try harder") tell you nothing you can act on.

A three-question read
  • Did they do what was asked? Count, format, completeness — against the instruction, not your assumption.
  • Where did it break? Comprehension, spelling, following steps, or stamina — name the kind.
  • What's one strength and one watch-point? Every page has both. Find them.

End with a single next step, not a lecture. One specific thing to practise, tied to what the page actually revealed, is worth more than a general push to do better.

Grounded in the Reggio Emilia principle of documentation — making a child's learning visible so it can be understood and supported.
