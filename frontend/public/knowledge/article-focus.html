The ten-minute focus session

Attention is a muscle, and muscles are built in short, repeated reps — not marathon sittings.

For ParentsDiscipline FocusRead 4 min

If getting your child to focus feels like a nightly battle, the problem is often not willpower — it is the length of the ask. Young attention spans are short by design, and the fastest way to lose a child is to demand more minutes than they have. The counter-intuitive fix is to ask for fewer.

A ten-minute session, done well and done daily, will do more for a child's focus over a month than an hour of resistance ever could. Here is why, and how.

Why ten minutes wins

Executive-function skills — holding an instruction in mind, resisting a distraction, staying with a task — are not things a child is simply born with. They develop through practice, at increasing levels of difficulty, much like physical training. Ten focused minutes is a rep. A frustrated forty-five is an injury: it teaches the child that this kind of work feels bad, and that lesson is the one that sticks.

Keep the session short enough that effort is still available when it ends.

How to run one

Anchor the work to something the child already loves. A child who resists "reading" will often lean into reading about dinosaurs, or building, or whatever their world is made of. Resistance drops sharply when new work is tied to an existing interest.

Give one instruction at a time, and ask them to repeat it back. Multi-step directions get lost; a single clear step keeps working memory free for the actual task. Make time visible — a simple timer turns an abstract "focus now" into a concrete finish line the child can see coming.

Try this week
  • Set a visible timer for ten minutes, tied to something they like.
  • One instruction at a time — have them repeat it back.
  • Praise starting, specifically and immediately ("you sat down and opened the book straight away").
  • Stop while there's still effort in the tank — end on a win, not a meltdown.

End before the tank is empty. Finishing on a small success is what makes a child willing to sit down again tomorrow — and tomorrow is where the growth actually happens.
