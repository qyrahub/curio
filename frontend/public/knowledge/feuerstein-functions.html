“Where is the traffic jam?”

A way of looking at a struggling learner that changes the question — from why aren't you getting this? to where, exactly, is this getting stuck?

For Parents & FamiliesDiscipline CognitiveRead 7 min
The core idea

Thinking is not one thing — it happens in three stages. Information is gathered, then processed, then expressed. When a child struggles, the breakdown is almost always in one specific stage, not in "ability." Find the stage, and you find something you can actually help with.

The psychologist Reuven Feuerstein built his life's work on a radical and hopeful premise: intelligence is not a fixed quantity you are issued at birth. It is modifiable. With the right mediation — an adult who steps in and guides the thinking, rather than just marking the answer — a child's cognitive functions can be built, strengthened and integrated over time.

The practical gift of his framework is diagnostic. It gives a parent a map for locating the jam.

The three stages of information processing

Stage 1

Input

Gathering. Taking the information in — accurately, completely, systematically.

→
Stage 2

Elaboration

Thinking. Working with what came in — defining, comparing, planning, testing.

→
Stage 3

Output

Expressing. Getting the answer out clearly, precisely, under control.

A child who "can't do comprehension" might have understood the passage perfectly and simply rushed the output. A child who "isn't trying" might never have taken the instruction in cleanly at Input. The mark on the page looks the same. The fix is completely different.

What each stage is made of

Each stage is built from specific, nameable functions. You don't need to memorise them — you need to recognise them when they wobble.

Input

Gathering the data

  1. Focus and perceiveUse the senses and the mind together.
  2. Search systematicallySeek with a method, not at random.
  3. Label thingsHave the words to think with.
  4. Know where you are in spaceLeft, right, front, back.
  5. Be aware of timeHow long, how often, in what order.
  6. Conserve constanciesKnow what stays the same when things change.
  7. Collect precise, accurate dataRight stuff in, right answer out.
  8. Use two or more sources at onceHold several things in mind together.

Elaboration

Working with the data

  1. Define the problemWhat am I actually being asked?
  2. Find the relevant cuesWhat matters — and what doesn't?
  3. CompareSee similarities and differences.
  4. Recall and use informationHold the picture in working memory.
  5. Understand realityLook for cause and effect, not fragments.
  6. Provide logical evidenceShow the working, not just the answer.
  7. Think abstractlyMove beyond the concrete; visualise.
  8. Use hypothetical thinkingIf this is true, what else follows?
  9. Test the hypothesisHow could I check this?
  10. Make a planThink forward: steps, and reasons.
  11. Sum upWhat is the main idea here?
  12. Form categoriesGroup things, and name the groups.

Output

Getting it out

  1. Consider another viewpointCognitive flexibility — what's it like in your shoes?
  2. Project relationshipsSee the non-obvious connection.
  3. Stick to itPerseverance; overcoming blocking.
  4. “Just a moment…”Pause instead of trial-and-error guessing.
  5. Give a thoughtful responseEngage mind before mouth.
  6. Use precision and accuracyTake the time; complete it properly.
  7. Transport it visuallyCopy accurately; keep things in order.
  8. Show self-controlThink before speaking or acting.

Coaching through a mental block

When a child is stuck, resist the urge to re-explain the content louder. Instead, work the three stages in order — and let the child hear you doing it. This is mediation: you are lending them your thinking process until it becomes theirs.

Step 1

Check the input

“Let's pause. What information do we actually have here? Let's read it together, carefully. What exactly is this question asking us to focus on?”
Step 2

Guide the elaboration

“What's our plan here? What have we done before that's similar? What details can we safely ignore?”
Step 3

Refine the output

“Read your answer out loud to me. Does it clearly explain your thinking to someone else — or should we check it against the question again?”

Don't ask “Why aren't you getting this?” Ask “Where is the traffic jam?”

An unfolding developmental process

These functions are not switches that are either on or off. They emerge and strengthen as a child grows — which is precisely why observing them over time matters more than judging them in a single moment.

Younger children (≈4–8)

Emerging

Cognitive functions are developing as the brain develops. Expect wobble; mediate generously.

→
Older students (≈9–18+)

Strengthening & integrating

Functions consolidate and combine with metacognition — thinking about their own thinking.

Why this sits inside Curio

Feuerstein's framework and Curio's method share a spine. Both refuse the verdict ("she's just not a maths person") in favour of the specific, locatable, workable observation ("the instruction never landed cleanly — that's an Input issue, and here's what we do about it"). Both hold that what a child can do is modifiable. And both insist that the way you find out is by watching carefully, over time, with someone who cares standing alongside.

That is what a review in Develop is doing when it names a watch-point rather than a score — and what the Brain is doing when it reads a child's history rather than ranking them against strangers.
